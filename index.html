Core Values in Labor
During labor, feeling safe, heard, informed, and respected helps build trust and allows you to stay actively involved in decisions about your care. These values support both emotional well-being and physical health.
Feeling Safe
Being in an environment where you trust the people caring for you and feel physically and emotionally secure. This sense of safety can help your body relax and support the natural flow of labor.
Feeling Heard
Your questions, concerns, and preferences are listened to and taken seriously. Open communication helps build trust and ensures your voice is part of every conversation.
Choices Being Respected
Your preferences are acknowledged and honored whenever possible. When plans change, you are included in the discussion and supported in making informed decisions.
Feeling Informed
Understanding what is happening in your body and why certain options are offered. Clear explanations empower you to make decisions with confidence throughout labor.
Provider Guidance
Recognizing and considering the knowledge, experience, and recommendations of your care team as part of shared decision-making during labor.
Everyone Staying Healthy
Focusing on the well-being of both the birthing person and the baby. This includes monitoring, timely care, and making adjustments when needed.

Comfort Measures
Comfort measures are the ways pain and discomfort are managed during labor. Some options support the body's natural coping abilities, while others use medical interventions. Many people choose a combination of both.
Overall Preference
Natural comfort techniques first
Medical comfort techniques first
Combination of both
Natural Comfort Measures
Non-medical techniques that work with your body's natural response to labor. Can be used alone or alongside medical comfort options.
Aromatherapy
Using familiar scents to promote calm, reduce nausea, and support relaxation.
Breathing Techniques
Slow, relaxed breathing helps calm the nervous system, manage pain, and support oxygen flow to both parent and baby.
Movement & Position Changes
Staying mobile can increase comfort, help baby move into a helpful position, and support labor progress.
Touch & Massage
Gentle touch, counterpressure, or hip squeezes can reduce pain signals and promote relaxation.
Attention Focusing / Ritual
Directing attention to a calming object, voice, or sensation to reduce awareness of pain.
Comfortable Environment
A space that feels private, comfortable, and respectful can help you relax and support the natural progress of birth.
Eat & Drink as Desired
Maintain energy, comfort, and hydration as needed.
Guided Visualizations
Uses calming imagery and focused imagination to help reduce stress and promote comfort during labor.
TENs Machine
Mild electrical pulses through pads on the lower back to help reduce labor discomfort and support natural pain management.
Medical Comfort Measures
Pain relief options involving medications or medical procedures, provided and managed by healthcare professionals.
Sterile Water Injections
Small injections in the lower back that can relieve intense back labor for a few hours.
Nitrous Oxide (Laughing Gas)
A self-administered gas that takes the edge off pain and anxiety during contractions without affecting baby.
Epidural / Spinal
Medication placed in the lower back that numbs pain from the waist down. Provides strong relief but limits movement.
Opioids
Help with relaxation and rest, especially in early labor, though may cause drowsiness or nausea.
General Anesthetic
Used in emergency C-sections when immediate action is needed. The parent is asleep for the birth.

Labor Interventions
Interventions in labor are medical procedures used to support safety, comfort, or progress during childbirth. They may be recommended based on how labor is unfolding or specific health needs.
Induction & Augmentation
Induction starts labor artificially when it has not begun on its own, while augmentation supports labor that is moving slowly.
Natural Methods
Gentle techniques like a membrane sweep, nipple stimulation, intimacy, or pumping to encourage labor naturally.
Medical Methods
Medications or tools to soften the cervix, start contractions, or break the water to help labor progress.
Augmentation
Support when labor has started but contractions slow or weaken. May involve medication or breaking the water.
Natural Progress
Contractions begin naturally and build in strength and rhythm. Pauses in labor are met with patience.
Other Interventions
IV Fluids
Fluids through IV to support hydration, deliver medications, or maintain fluid balance.
Continuous Fetal Monitoring (CTG)
Continuously records the baby's heart rate and the pattern of contractions during labor.
Intermittent Monitoring (Doppler)
Listens to baby's heartbeat every 15–30 minutes with a handheld doppler. Standard in low-risk pregnancies.
Vaginal Birth
Spontaneous Vaginal Delivery
Baby delivered through the vagina without instruments or surgical procedures.
VBAC
Attempting a vaginal birth after a prior C-section.
Episiotomy
A small cut at the vaginal opening to create more space. Rarely needed today and usually avoided unless absolutely necessary.
Forceps Delivery
A tool placed around baby's head to guide them out while the parent pushes. Sometimes used to avoid a C-section.
Vacuum Delivery
A soft suction cup on baby's head to assist with birth during pushing. Used when help is needed and birth is close.
Surgical Birth / C-Section
A C-section is a surgical procedure to deliver a baby through an incision in the abdomen and uterus, performed when a vaginal birth may not be safe.
Planned C-Section
Scheduled before labor begins, often for medical reasons or personal preference.
Emergency C-Section
Performed when complications arise during labor that make a vaginal birth unsafe.
Repeat C-Section
A C-section for someone who has had a previous C-section.
Gentle / Family-Centered C-Section
Modifications to include immediate bonding, skin-to-skin, or more involvement from the birthing parent and family.

The Golden Hour
The golden hour is the first 1–2 hours after birth — an important time for bonding, warmth, and adjustment to life outside the womb. Skin-to-skin contact and early feeding can help regulate the baby's temperature, heart rate, and breathing.
Immediate Bonding
Skin-to-Skin — Birthing Parent
Skin-to-Skin — Partner
Breastfeeding
Bottle Feeding
Placenta Options
The delivery of the placenta is the final stage of labor. For many people, it also holds personal or cultural significance.
Expectant Management
The placenta is born naturally as the body continues contracting.
Active Management
Medication to help the uterus contract, and the placenta is guided out quickly — usually within about 15 minutes.
Take Placenta Home
For personal, cultural, or ceremonial reasons.
View the Placenta
Umbilical Cord
The timing of cord clamping can influence iron levels, circulation, and early adjustment to life outside the womb.
Immediate Clamping
The cord is clamped and cut within the first few seconds after birth.
Delayed Clamping
Clamping delayed (often 30–60 seconds or longer) to allow continued blood flow from the placenta.
Physiologic Clamping
The cord is clamped after it naturally stops pulsing.
Cord Blood Collection
Blood remaining in the cord and placenta collected for private or public banking.
Partner Cuts the Cord
Newborn Assessments & Procedures
Many of these assessments can be done while baby remains on the parent's chest, when both are stable, to support bonding during the golden hour.
Assessments on Parent's Chest
General assessments and measurements while on parent's chest.
Assessments at Warmer
Baby taken to warmer for general assessments and measurements.
Vitamin K
Given after birth to help prevent rare but serious bleeding problems by supporting normal blood clotting.
Erythromycin
Helps prevent bacterial eye infections that can be passed to the baby during delivery.
RSV Immunization
Protects newborns from severe respiratory syncytial virus infections, especially when born during cold and flu season.
Hepatitis B Vaccine
Usually recommended within 24 hours of birth if the birthing parent has hepatitis B or status is unknown.
Drying & Stimulation
May help the baby begin breathing and maintain body temperature more quickly.
Minimal Drying & Stimulation
If the baby is stable and you wish to prioritize uninterrupted skin-to-skin contact.

Personal Care & Considerations
Each person's needs, preferences, and circumstances are unique. Respectful, personalised support ensures you feel informed, supported, and empowered throughout your birth.
Trauma-Informed Care
When a person with a history of trauma gives birth, the experience can be overwhelming. Trauma-informed care focuses on safety, control, trust, and empowerment.
Consent & Autonomy
Any intervention should involve explicit consent.
Control
Freedom to change positions, movement, and pace of pushing.
Trauma-Informed Staff
Staff trained to recognize and respond to triggers. They ask permission before touch or procedures and offer choices when possible.
Privacy
A secure and personal space with limited interruptions and a respectful environment.
Emotional Care
Clear Communication
Providers explain all procedures, ask permission, and give choices.
Continuous Support
A doula or provider who can help manage anxiety, advocate for you, and provide reassurance and coping strategies.
Positive Self-Talk
Encouraging and reassuring phrases to help build confidence, reduce fear, and stay focused during contractions.
